01
The week before

I sort what actually sells

My objective: three piles — sell, donate, toss — decided before the tables ever come out. The number one yard-sale mistake is putting everything out and hoping. The number two mistake is skipping the five-minute check of your city's yard-sale rules — most Valley cities allow a few per year, and some want a quick online permit.
Sells every time

Tools, furniture, kitchen, kids

Hand and power tools, solid furniture, working kitchenware, kids' gear and toys, garden equipment, sporting goods, records and books in good shape. If it works and it's clean, it has a buyer.

Donate or toss instead

Worn clothes, old tech, broken anything

Used clothing barely moves outside baby sizes and brands. Old TVs and printers are e-waste, not inventory. Anything broken or stained costs table space that a $[amount] tool would have earned. Donate it now and keep the receipt.

Why the sort is the profitShoppers judge the whole sale in one slow drive-by. Ten good tables pull people out of cars; ten good tables plus five junk tables reads as junk. Curation is merchandising — the same reason I stage a house before I list it.
Powerful ally · Curation
Deciding what NOT to sell is the first sale you make.

It's the same discipline as a listing: what you leave out shapes what everything else is worth. A curated driveway earns more than a crowded one, exactly like a decluttered house shows better than a full one.

02
The night before

I price to move and stage to sell

What you are watching: a client asked me to help set up their pre-move sale. The pricing rule that changes everything: you're not pricing what it cost you or what it's worth to you — you're pricing for the person standing in your driveway at 8am with cash. That's a fraction of retail, and making peace with that the night before is what makes the day work.
Kareem ↔ Homeowner clientSetup night
HO
This dresser was $[amount] new. I can't put $[low price] on it — that feels insulting.
I know. But retail died the day it left the store. Tomorrow it's competing with free sofas on the curb. Price it to be gone by 10am, and remember what you're really buying: an empty garage and a lighter move.
Why I reframe the goalThe dresser isn't the asset — the space is. Sellers who price on sentiment take it all back inside at 2pm. Priced right, the stuff leaves AND pays for the pizza. This is the same talk I give about list price, at 1/1000th the stakes.
Setup rules: everything on tables at eye level, nothing on tarps. Sticker every single item — unpriced means unasked. Big items out front by the curb, they're the billboard. And have $[amount] in small bills and quarters for change.
HO
What about signs? I was going to post online only.
Do both. Online listings the night before with photos of your best items — and big, fat-marker signs with arrows at the nearest busy corners, up by 6:30am. Half your buyers never saw the listing; they saw an arrow.
Why signage is marketingA yard sale is a one-day listing: photos online, signage on the street, best assets out front. If this sounds like how I launch a house, that's because it is.
Powerful ally · Presentation
Price to the market, stage the merchandise, market the event.

Every rule of a good listing shows up in a good yard sale — priced to where buyers actually are, displayed at its best, and advertised where the traffic is. Small scale, same craft.

Yard sale setup checklist: - Sort into sell / donate / toss the week before. Check your city's yard-sale rules. - Price everything with stickers — unpriced means unasked. Coffee. Comfortable shoes. - Midday markdowns planned in advance; donation run booked for the same afternoon.
03
Sale day · the fun part

I handle the haggle

What you are watching: every price at a yard sale is an opening offer, and that's not rude — it's the culture of the thing. The strategy is simple: hold a floor on your few big items, bundle instead of discounting, and say yes fast on everything small. Velocity beats margin in a one-day market.
Kareem ↔ Yard-sale shopperLive in the driveway
SH
Would you take $[offer] for the drill? Tag says $[price].
Tell you what — the drill's priced fair because it works and it comes with the case. But grab the sander next to it too and I'll do both for $[bundle]. Deal?
The bundle counterNever just discount — attach. A bundle protects the price of the good item, moves a second item, and the shopper still feels like they won. This is seller-credit logic in miniature: give value that costs you less than it's worth to them.
SH
Hmm. What's the lowest you'd go on just the drill?
$[floor] and that's honest — you've seen what these run used online. But the bundle's the better deal, and I'll even throw in the box of bits.
Name the floor once, kindlyOn the handful of items with real value, I give one firm number with a reason, then add a sweetener instead of a second cut. Chasing a haggler downward teaches every listener in the driveway that your tags mean nothing.
SH
Fine, the bundle. And what about this mug — a quarter?
Sold. Want a second mug free? They're divorced from their set and I'm not driving them anywhere tonight.
Say yes fast on small stuffThe quarter isn't the point — the momentum is. Fast, cheerful yeses keep people browsing, and browsers buy. Know which items deserve a negotiation and which deserve a smile.
Powerful ally · Negotiation
Anchors, bundles, floors, and sweeteners — sound familiar?

Every move in this driveway is a move from my escrow playbook: protect the valuable thing, trade value that costs you little, and never negotiate against yourself. If you like how I haggle over a drill, you'll like how I haggle over your house.

04
The characters you'll meet

I keep the day friendly

What you are watching: every yard sale gets the same cast — early birds, lowballers, dealers, and hagglers-for-sport. None of them are villains. Each one gets a calm answer that protects your morning without souring it.
EB
*knocking at 6:40am* Saw the listing — can I look before you open?
We open at 8 sharp — but leave me your number and first thing you're hunting, and if it's here I'll text you a photo at 7:30. Fair?
LB
I'll give you a dollar for the $[price] lamp. Cash, right now.
Ha — cash was already the plan, friend. The lamp's $[price] this morning and half that after noon. Come back at 12 and gamble that it's still here.
DL
I'll take the whole box of records for $[low offer]. Save you the trouble.
Appreciate the offer — dealers have good eyes, which tells me the box is worth more. Pick your five favorites at tag price, and if the box is still here at close-out, we'll talk bulk for real.
SH
It's scratched right here. Half price?
You're right, it is — that's why it's already priced at a tenth of new. The scratch is in the tag. I'll do $[small cut] off because you looked closely, and that's the number.
Powerful ally · Composure
A dealer's interest is information, not an insult.

When a professional wants your whole box cheap, they just appraised it for you. The same is true when an investor lowballs your house — the offer tells you the asset is worth chasing. Read the signal, keep the floor, stay friendly.

2pm · nothing goes back inside

I close out the same day

The step everyone skips: the sale isn't over when the shoppers thin out — it's over when the driveway is empty and nothing went back in the garage. Markdowns at noon, a free box at the curb, and the donation run booked before the sale even started.
Kareem ↔ Homeowner clientClose-out, 2pm
HO
We made $[total]! Should we box the rest for a sale next month?
Congratulations — and no. The rest goes in the truck now: donation center closes at 5, and grab the itemized receipt. Ask your CPA about the deduction — that's their lane, but the receipt is free either way. The garage stays empty tonight.
